Commercial Cases of FCC Bio Co-processing and Carbon Reduction Effects

Introduction

FCC (Fluid Catalytic Cracking) bio co-processing involves blending biogenic feedstocks with conventional petroleum feedstocks in existing refinery units to produce lower-carbon intensity fuels. This approach leverages existing infrastructure while reducing the carbon footprint of transportation fuels.

Key Commercial Cases and Their Carbon Reduction Effects

1. Honeywell UOP and Preem Collaboration

Commercial Case: Honeywell UOP conducted a commercial co-processing trial with Preem involving biomass-based pyrolysis oil in an FCC unit to produce partially renewable transportation fuel.

Carbon Reduction Effect: - The technology demonstrates a net (Scope 1-3) CO₂ reduction of 3.8 wt% per year by co-processing 5.4 wt% (5 vol%) renewable feedstock - Co-processing lipidic and cellulosic-derived bio-oils can produce low-carbon fuels with significant emissions reductions

Source: Honeywell UOP Co-Processing Technologies brochure

2. Petrobras and NREL Demonstration Project

Commercial Case: Petrobras conducted extensive co-processing experiments with pine-derived bio-oils and Brazilian vacuum gasoil (VGO) on their 200 kg/h demonstration-scale FCC unit.

Carbon Reduction Effect: - When co-processing 20% bio-oil, renewable carbon content in gasoline cuts varied between 3% and 5% - For 10% bio-oil in feed, approximately 2% renewable carbon content achieved - Techno-economic analysis shows feasibility at crude oil prices of $70/barrel using up to 5 wt% bio-oil

Sources: ScienceDirect, Petrobras begins producing fuels with cellulosic content

3. Neste Renewable Hydrocarbons

Commercial Case: Neste has developed commercial-scale renewable hydrocarbon production through various processing routes.

Carbon Reduction Effect: - Life cycle assessment shows greenhouse gas emission reduction of more than 85% when replacing conventional fossil feedstock - Neste RE renewable hydrocarbons demonstrate substantial carbon footprint reduction across the value chain

Source: Neste life cycle assessment news release

4. Repsol and Bunge Intermediate Crops Project

Commercial Case: Repsol and Bunge collaborate to process intermediate crops (camelina and safflower) into low-carbon intensity oils for renewable diesel and SAF production.

Carbon Reduction Effect: - Emission reductions of up to 90% compared to conventional diesel - Expansion of low-carbon intensity feedstock pool for renewable fuel production

Source: Repsol press release

5. Waste Cooking Oil (WCO) Co-processing

Commercial Case: Multiple refiners are implementing WCO co-processing in FCC units, as promoted by technology providers like W.R. Grace.

Carbon Reduction Effect: - SAF made from UCO achieves 45%-90% reduction in carbon intensity - Typical restaurant producing 200 gallons of UCO monthly can generate LCFS credits worth $100-200 per metric ton of CO₂ reduced - Co-processing WCO represents a significant step toward circular economy and carbon reduction

Sources: W.R. Grace, Biofuels-news.com

6. FCC Propylene Co-processing

Commercial Case: Biofeed co-processing in FCC units to produce low-carbon propylene.

Carbon Reduction Effect: - Co-processing rate of 10 wt% renewable feed can reduce carbon intensity of resulting propylene by 9% - FCC-produced propylene already has favorable carbon intensity compared to other processes

Source: DigitalRefining article on biofeed FCC co-processing

Challenges and Considerations

The search results indicate several important considerations: - Technical challenges: Oxygen content in bio-oils can cause operational issues, corrosion, and catalyst deactivation - Economic viability: Highly dependent on crude oil prices, incentives, and regulatory frameworks - Carbon accounting: Accurate tracking of biogenic carbon content is essential for credit generation - Feedstock compatibility: Different bio-feeds require specific processing strategies

Conclusion

FCC bio co-processing has demonstrated significant commercial progress with measurable carbon reduction effects ranging from approximately 3% to over 90%, depending on the feedstock type, co-processing rate, and specific technology applied. The technology represents a practical pathway for refiners to decarbonize existing operations while leveraging current infrastructure. The carbon reduction benefits are substantiated through life cycle assessments, demonstration projects, and commercial-scale implementations across multiple major energy companies.

Note: Carbon reduction effects vary based on feedstock type, processing conditions, and methodology used for carbon accounting.
